Section 1.0

The charts in this section are designed to help you understand how your home’s current energy use compares with UK averages and targets. We also show the extent to which your home could be improved to meet the UK 2050 80% carbon reduction target, reduce your fuel bills and improve comfort.

We’ve modelled your home as it is now in SAP (Standard Assessment Procedure). We’ve modelled it again to show the effect of the measures proposed in Section 2.0 of this report.

Where provided, we’ve also compared this with information from your utility bills. The SAP model makes a set of assumptions, based on averages, about climate, heating patterns, hot water and appliance use. If your energy bill data is from a particularly mild or cold year, of if your household habits are different from the UK average, the model may produce a predicted energy use that is different from your actual energy use.

Through the way you live in your home, or changes you’ve already made, you may already be some way towards the 2050 target. For example, you may use your space heating a lot less than is assumed in the model, or have your thermostat at the lowest setting.

However, if you are significantly under-heating your home, you may have noticed other problems such as excessive condensation or poor indoor air quality. This may affect your comfort and health - and is something you might want to tackle through energy efficiency retrofit work.

Alternatively, you may find that you are using far more energy than predicted in the SAP model, and that a few simple steps could help reduce this significantly. Likewise, if you continue to use a lot of electricity – with lots of high demand electrical appliances such as tumble driers or plasma TVs – then you are likely to still use more energy after retrofit than suggested by the SAP model.

1.1 Heat losses and gains

In the UK, most homes use the majority of energy to keep warm during the cooler months of winter, spring and autumn. When it is colder outside, your home continuously loses heat from the warmer inside through its building fabric. The building fabric is made up of walls, floor, roof and windows. The better insulated your home the slower this heat loss happens.

Your home also loses heat through draughts and ventilation losses. This is when warm air leaves your home unintentionally through gaps in the building fabric – or intentionally through windows or fans. This rate of heat loss depends on how draughty your home is and the ways you choose to ventilate your home.

1.2 Space heating demand

To keep your home warm, the gap between heat losses and heat gains in your home needs to be topped up by your heating system. The amount of energy needed to do this is known as space heating demand.

Space heating demand is measured in kilowatt-hours per square metre per year, so that different sizes of building can be compared directly. Space heating demand takes into account all the heat loss and heat gain factors described in 1.1 above.

Space heating demand does not cover any other energy uses, such as lighting, hot water or electricity use for fans, pumps and appliances. It does take into account the type of controls used by your heating system, and how advanced they are. It is a good way of understanding the performance of the fabric of your home, and how energy efficient it is.

If your home is uncomfortably cold or you have problems with condensation and damp, the most important thing you can do is reduce space heating demand by improving the efficiency of your building fabric. This will also improve the comfort and health of your home.

1.4 Primary energy use

Primary energy is the total amount of energy used by your home, from the power station to your lightbulb. Primary energy is measured in kilowatt-hours per square metre per year.

The level of insulation in your home, the efficiency of services such as heating and lighting, and the type of fuel used all affect your primary energy use.

The same task, such as boiling a kettle, will take different amounts of primary energy depending on the fuel used. This is because of efficiency differences in the way various fuels are generated and distributed. Primary energy includes an allowance for the efficiency of the national electricity grid and fuel transport.

1.5 Carbon dioxide emissions

The carbon dioxide emissions from your home are measured in kilograms per square metre per year. Carbon dioxide emissions are affected by the level of insulation, the efficiency of services such as heating and lighting, and by the type of fuel used for each of the services in your home.

For example, with the current make up of the national grid, 1 kilowatt hour of mains electricity produces more carbon dioxide than 1 kilowatt hour of mains gas.

1.7 SAP rating

SAP is the Standard Assessment Procedure used to assess energy use in UK homes. The UK government devised SAP as a way to compare homes and test them against Building Regulations. SAP forms the basis of the energy model used in this report.

SAP makes assumptions about the number of people living in a home and how it is heated and used, based on averages for the UK. You may not be ‘average’ and may use your home in different ways. So, SAP is not an exact predictive tool.

The final SAP rating is influenced by predicted energy use, carbon emissions and fuel costs. This means that the SAP rating is influenced by levels of insulation, how efficient your heating system and other services are, the type of fuel you use to heat and light your home, and whether you have any renewables. SAP does not cover appliances or cooking.

SAP is not a perfect system. A home with a poor building fabric could still get a good rating because it has lots of renewables or energy costs are cheap. This is unhelpful if energy conservation or comfort is your biggest concern.

This is why we’ve given SAP a lower level of priority within this report. The table below shows the SAP rating we’ve calculated for your home now, and in the various scenarios mapped out in this report.

1.8 Comfort and Health

The link between the built environment, our comfort and health is well established. Living in a home that is too cold, hot, damp, dry or draughty can affect your physical and mental wellbeing. This may trigger or worsen respiratory and cardiovascular problems, skin conditions, trips and falls, anxiety and depression.

When calculating heating demand, SAP assumes a temperature of 21°C in your living area for a set number of hours. In reality, your living area may be warmer or cooler than this (for longer or shorter periods) because we all have different perceptions of what is comfortable as well as different occupancy patterns. This will also be affected by the efficiency of the building fabric and your heating system.

This is why we collect information about you as householders. Understanding your perceptions of comfort, as well as how you use the spaces within your home, allows us to tailor the recommendations with your comfort and health in mind.

3.1 Project brief

The first step is for you to develop your own project brief. This might be something fairly simple, such as: top up loft insulation, draught-proof windows, fit new boiler.

For more extensive and complicated works, it is worth taking the time to develop a more detailed brief. Writing this yourself will help you get your thoughts in order. You can then use the project brief to communicate your needs to any professionals you work with.

Your project brief should include:

  1. Your aims:

    why you’re doing the work and your priorities in terms of comfort, bill savings and carbon emissions reduction. Also consider how the project fits in with other planned works, e.g. loft extensions, kitchen refurbishments, etc.

  2. The scope of works:

    what you want to do and how much work is involved

  3. Quality expectations:

    what standard you expect work to be completed to, giving examples if needed (i.e. fully finished and decorated, or part DIY and critical elements for energy performance)

  4. Your budget:

    if you have a fixed pot of money it is best to be explicit about this up front though you may want to explore your options further before you set a budget

  5. Your programme:

    when you want to do the works, whether you have any fixed deadlines, and whether you want to do everything all at once or staged over time.

  6. Your priorities:

    in all building projects there is a balance between risks around time, cost and quality. It’s best to be clear about which of these is most important to you, as it’s unlikely you’ll be able to achieve highly in all three areas. Please see Figure 20 below.

  7. Your contingencies:

    what is your backup if things don’t go completely to plan? For example, what is essential and what could you remove if things go over budget. Can the work be staged?

Your project brief is a living document that will change as the process develops. Any consultant you work with will understand this and should be able to help you.

3.2 Packaging works

If you are carrying out the works in phases, it will be important to decide the order that will work best for you and also achieve the maximum benefits from each of the measures.

You may wish to focus on those measures that have the greatest impact on your priorities, or those that are partly funded under current grant regimes or subsidies. Do consider how the impact of some measures are dependent on the order in which they are done. There are a few simple rules to keep in mind when considering how you package the works:

  1. Reduce demand before adding supply:

    Environmentally and economically, it is often better to reduce overall energy demand before considering energy supply measures. For example, before you invest several thousand pounds in photo-voltaic panels, make sure you’ve:

    1. Insulated everywhere you can;

    2. Draught-proofed your home;

    3. Fitted low-energy lightbulbs and appliances;

    4. Got an up-to-date and efficient boiler with good controls that minimise energy use;

    5. Installed low-flow hot water fittings and use the most efficient means of heating your hot water available in your home.

  2. Keep fabric improvements together:

    If you’re considering fabric improvements, such as external wall insulation and new windows, try to make sure they’re done at the same time. This will minimise thermal bridging to reduce heat losses and maximise the effectiveness of fabric improvements. Thermal bridging is when a gap in the insulation layer happens at the junction between two different building elements. The same goes for internal wall insulation and floor insulation.

    Carrying out these measures at the same time will also reduce the overall amount of disruption caused to your home. The calculations in this report assume thermal bridging is reduced, to maximise savings.

  3. Consider health and comfort:

    Retrofit is not just about saving energy, money or carbon. It is also about improving the health and comfort of your home. If you currently suffer from cold and damp, then improving the insulation of your building fabric may be a priority for you. By doing this you will make your home more comfortable and save carbon.

    If you improve the air-tightness of your home through retrofit works, or if you currently have problems with build up of condensation and poor indoor air-quality, then you should prioritise improvements to the way your home is ventilated and / or make better, energy efficient provision for things like drying laundry.

  4. Consider maintenance needs and general upgrades:

    It’s likely that some of the works we’ve suggested for your property will need to be done in some form anyway, as part of future maintenance.

    For example, you may need to upgrade old doors and windows that are coming to the end of their useful life and are broken or rotten. When doing this, one of your criteria for choosing replacements should be their energy performance. If you’re already going to the trouble of having things replaced or upgraded, this can make a lot of sense as the additional cost or hassle of significant energy improvements can be minimal.

3.3 Regulations and approvals

Building works on your home will often require formal and statutory approvals. It is important to make sure you follow the rules here because not doing so may cause problems later – for example when you come to sell your home but don’t have the proper approvals in place for any works done.

  1. Planning permission:

    Any measures that change the external appearance, size or shape of your home may require planning permission. You may also need Listed Building consent or Conservation Area approval. You should seek further advice from your Local Planning Authority.

  2. Building Regulations:

    These are mostly concerned with Health and Safety and structural integrity. Work that affects the energy performance of your home, electrical installations and structural alterations or fire planning will require Building Regulations approval.

    In some cases, such as with windows and boiler installations, it is possible to bypass this by using recognised certification schemes and approved installers (for example Gas Safe for boilers and FENSA for windows). You should seek further advice from Local Authority Building Control.

    More information on both planning and Building Control is available on the Planning Portal website: www.planningportal.gov.uk

  3. Party Wall consent:

    If you are carrying out work on a wall or structure that forms the boundary between your property and a neighbouring property then you need to make sure that the work doesn’t adversely affect your neighbour. This is important for you to be able to maintain a good relationship with your neighbour.

    The processes around Party Wall consent cover this. You can find out more about this here: www.gov.uk/guidance/party-wall-etc-act-1996-guidance

  4. Mortgage lenders and building insurance:

    If you are carrying out works on your home then it’s important that you inform both your mortgage lender and your building insurance company. They may have specific standards that they require you to meet, or particular measures they need you to take. For example, you may need to take specific measures around security to ensure your insurance remains valid.

3.4 Assembling your team

For your retrofit to be a success, it is important that you’re able to bring together the right team of people. This will help you get what you want and get good value out of the process. Your team could include the following:

  1. Specialists and consultants:

    Before carrying out some of the more extensive building works outlined above, detailed drawings and technical specifications should be prepared and agreed.

    This is especially important when major changes are being made to the fabric of your home or its services. For example, insulation and air-tightness works should consider moisture movement, thermal bridges in the fabric and indoor air quality. This should avoid problems with condensation and damp and reduce the risk of poor air quality affecting your health.

    Often this will be achieved by commissioning a consultant or specialist to develop the designs, providing independent advice that is backed up by professional experience and accreditations. These professionals may go on to oversee the implementation of these works, or they may stop before this and simply provide design information.

    Professionals involved in your project may include architects, building surveyors, clerks of works, energy advisors, services/mechanical and electrical engineers. Consider who will bring the most value to your project, so that the costs of professional fees are worthwhile. For example, a good services engineer can advise on complex heating and renewable systems, which may save money in installation and running costs, but you probably wouldn’t need to appoint them to specify a new standard gas boiler.

  2. Builders and contractors:

    There are some works that you can probably do without a contractor, for example installing draught proofing, new lightbulbs or water-saving fittings.

    However, other works may require the services of a professional builder. There are some specialist items that can only be installed by properly approved and accredited contractors.

    Not all builders are the same. They range from micro-builders, who perform simple multi-trade works from the back of a van, to huge publicly listed companies. It is important to get the right contractor for your job, who can do the work you want to the right standard.

    Some builders and contractors also provide design services, so to some degree they could take the place of the consultants listed above. However, it is important to consider whether this is the kind of service you want and to understand that their advice may not be fully independent.

3.5 Selection process and contracts

There are various formal means for selecting consultants and contractors. The same basic rules apply to all:

  • Work out what you need and list out the services you require from each party – making sure there are no gaps.

  • Get quotes from more than one source wherever possible, so that you can make comparisons.

  • Take up references, talk to past clients and visit past projects wherever possible.

  • Check they have the appropriate insurances – professional indemnity for consultants, public liability and contracts insurance for contractors.

  • Meet them and show them around your home – make sure you feel comfortable about working with them and can communicate well with them.

  • Remember your brief and priorities, and make sure your proposed team understands them – you’re not obliged to take the lowest bidder!

  • If you’re not sure, re-tender and find someone else!

In all instances, once you’ve chosen your consultant or contractor, make sure you get a formal agreement in writing. This makes sure that you’re both clear on what they are expected to do.

You can get standard forms for both consultant appointments and building contracts from various professional and construction industry bodies. Any professionals that you appoint should be able to advise you on this.

3.6 Summary

There are four key points to remember when procuring your works:

  • Write a good clear brief for your project – outline what you want and what your expectations are before work starts.

  • In your project brief, include a clear understanding of your priorities and your backup plans – where you can compromise and where you cannot, what your contingencies are if things don’t go to plan.

  • Be clear about roles and responsibilities and who is doing what within your team – and don’t interfere too much. Once you’ve appointed them and told them what you want, let the professionals get on with it.

  • Spend time and effort planning the works and putting together the right team. Don’t get too frustrated by time spent on this – it will all be worth it in the end!

Appendix D: Glossary

Retrofit and building efficiency have a language all of their own. In this report there may be words and concepts that are unfamiliar to you. If there is anything you don’t understand, please ask us – either in person, through one of the regular Carbon Co-op webinars, or on the member forum.

We believe in using the correct terminology wherever possible, so that when you speak to professionals and tradespeople involved in retrofit you are familiar with the terms. To help your understanding we have provided this short glossary of common words and phrases:

  1. Air changes per hour (ACH):

    is usually used as a measure of ventilation but when natural ventilation is deployed the air-tightness contributes to the total.

  2. Air tightness:

    A draughty house allows heat to be lost around the insulation you have installed. Air tightness is usually measured as the volume of air that moves through a square metre of outside surface in an hour at a given difference in pressure between inside and outside – metres3/hour/metre2 at 50 Pascals, shorthand – q50.

  3. Kilowatt-hour (kWh):

    This is a unit of energy – a measure of the total amount of work done over a period of time. 1 kilowatt-hour would be used by a 1kW rated electric fire left on for one hour. A 100W bulb left on for one hour would use 0.1kWh.

  4. Regulated emissions:

    These are the energy uses and resulting carbon dioxide emissions that are covered by Building Regulations in the SAP rating (see below). These include the energy used to heat, light and ventilate your home and provide hot water.

  5. Retrofit:

    New houses can be built to a very high level of energy efficiency. Older houses, built when our expectations of comfort were much lower and when energy was cheaper, tend to perform very poorly in terms of energy efficiency, carbon emissions and comfort. These homes therefore need to be augmented with energy saving measures – both in fabric and services - or renewable energy generating measures, to bring them up to a more acceptable standard. This process is known as retrofit, i.e. ‘retrospectively fitting’ measures to an existing property.

  6. SAP:

    SAP is the Standard Assessment Procedure used to assess energy use in UK homes. The UK government devised SAP as a way to compare homes and test them against Building Regulations. SAP forms the basis of the energy model used in this report.

    SAP makes assumptions about the number of people living in a home and how it is heated and used, based on averages for the UK. You may not be ‘average’ and may use your home in different ways. So, SAP is not an exact predictive tool.

  7. SAP model:

    The SAP model is the tool used to calculate the energy and carbon performance of a home and produce its SAP rating. It uses a standard set of calculations, set out in publicly available documents.

  8. SAP rating:

    Domestic buildings are given a SAP rating in the Building Regulations approval process for new-build homes. Energy Performance Certificates (EPCs) for sales and lettings of existing homes also include a SAP rating. The higher the SAP rating, the better the building is in terms of energy efficiency, carbon dioxide emissions and fuel costs.

    The SAP rating is influenced by predicted energy use, carbon emissions and fuel costs. This means that the SAP rating is influenced by levels of insulation, how efficient your heating system and other services are, the type of fuel you use to heat and light your home, and whether you have any renewables. However, the SAP rating only covers regulated emissions. SAP does not cover appliances or cooking.

  9. Unregulated emissions:

    These are the energy uses and resulting carbon dioxide emissions that are not covered by Building Regulations. They are less directly related to the building fabric and include cooking and electrical appliance use. Appliance use covers laundry washing and drying, fridges and freezers, and consumer electricals such as TVs and computers.

  10. U-value:

    This is a measure of the thermal performance of a building element. U-value is measured in watts per square metre per degree of temperature difference (measured in Kelvin). So, if a wall has a U-value of 1 W/m2. K, it loses 1 watt per square metre of wall area per degree of difference between inside and outside. So, if it is 0oC outside and 20 oC inside and the wall is 10 square metres, it would lose 200 watts of heat – equivalent to the power used by a set of hair-straighteners.

  11. Watt:

    This is a unit of power – that is the rate at which work is done or energy is used. 1 watt is equivalent to 1 joule per second. A 100-watt lightbulb uses 100 joules per second. 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000W.

Appendix E. About our methodology

This report is based on a survey of the condition of your home at a fixed point in time (or as noted otherwise if you have planned works).

The information we gathered from this survey was entered into an energy model based on the UK’s National Calculation Methodology – SAP version 9.92.

However, this model is only a representation that allows different approaches to be compared – it does not produce guaranteed absolute predictions. Whilst changing the physical properties of your home as suggested in this report should make a positive contribution towards energy saving, the actual amount of energy saved depends on how you use and interact with your home.

We have evidence from previous retrofits that these measures work – but they will produce different outcomes for different households. Different types of construction will also not always perform in accordance with the models (for example solid walls may actually perform better than predicted in standard heat loss calculations).

We are confident that the levels of insulation we propose will have a significant impact on energy use and comfort. However, services measures are more difficult to predict, and the use of electrical appliances, hot water and cooking varies significantly between households.

Before making a final decision about retrofit works, you should carefully consider what your aims are, how they can best be achieved, and how you compare to the modelled average. Carbon Co-op can also help with advice on simple changes to how you use your home to save energy without altering the fabric.
